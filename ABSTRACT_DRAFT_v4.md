# ABSTRACT (v4 — synchronized with Core Document v3)

We present a cross-substrate structural invariant, Δ(Δ₁∅Δ₂), with seven components — three constrained variables (ω₁, ω₂, ω₃), a structural vacancy (∅), a quadratic constraint surface, an irreversible residue (Π), and a constituted third element (Object Δ) — that repeat in the same structural roles across five physically distinct substrates (seven entries). The formula is instantiated in the electrochemical memristor (Knowm SDC), the grezistor (two ruby crystals coupled via chromium-fluorescence photon exchange through an air gap), the NV-center qubit, the discrete time crystal (NV under periodic drive), and FM/NM/FM spintronics multilayers — the last realizing three sub-instantiations (GMR, TMR, STO) on a single material platform and providing retrospective structural calibration through experimentally established physics (Giant Magnetoresistance, Fert 1988, Grünberg 1989).

Three cross-substrate results emerge from comparing the five instantiations as a family. First, the vacancy ∅ between the two distinguished systems is polymorphic: it takes three topologically distinct forms — spatial (ℝ³), configurational (S²), and temporal (S¹). Each topology locks the bifurcation class (deterministic, stochastic, and discrete respectively) and the residue channel count (1, 2, and 3 respectively). Second, the autonomy of the constituted third element (Object Δ) is governed by the carrier's copyability: classical carriers produce direct autonomy, quantum carriers produce inverted autonomy, and semiclassical carriers produce conditional autonomy with a collective threshold. Third, these discrete invariants (channel count, autonomy sign, bifurcation class) are integer-valued, transfer across substrates sharing a vacancy topology, and are individually falsifiable. The cusp catastrophe of Thom is recovered as a limiting case (spatial vacancy, single epoch, no residue accumulation). A selection bias test against four additional systems not used in development (Josephson junction, electrochemical cell, photonic crystal cavity, synaptic cleft) yields 3/4 matches.

The framework generates 10 sharp, experimentally testable predictions (Tier 1), supplemented by 6 entries of decreasing strength (Tiers 2–4), totaling 16 independent entries. Of the 10 core predictions, four are testable with existing equipment (memristor aging curve, memristor switching asymmetry, TMR I-V asymmetry correlation, topological rigidity of bifurcation class), three require constructing the grezistor, and three require extreme conditions or specialized apparatus; none contradicts known physics.

The framework connects five physically distinct substrates through a common structural invariant without replacing their per-substrate physics (Valet-Fert, LLGS, Lindblad, Floquet). The quadratic constraint form is postulated, not derived from first principles. A preliminary steady-state fit to published Co/Cu GMR data (Appendix H) reproduces the measured spacer-thickness dependence at three antiferromagnetic coupling peaks to within ~20% using published material parameters with no additional free parameters; full dynamical validation is deferred.
